World Life Expectancy Dashboard: Uncovering Global Health Patterns Through Data

Project Overview

This Excel dashboard explores global life expectancy trends from 2000 to 2022 using visual insights drawn from various factors like GDP, BMI, adult mortality, and development status. The aim was to reveal patterns that explain why people in some countries live longer than others — and how economic or health indicators might play a role.

Objectives

• Compare life expectancy between developed and developing countries
• Analyze the influence of GDP and BMI on average life expectancy
• Identify countries with the highest and lowest adult mortality
• Provide a visual snapshot for data-driven storytelling

Tools Used

• MySQL (Data cleaning, transformations, and queries)
• Microsoft Excel (KPI cards, bar charts, combo charts, dashboard layout)
• PivotTables, conditional formatting, and custom charting

Key Insights

Average Life Expectancy by Development Status

• Developed countries show an average of 79.2 years, while developing countries average 66.8 years.
• This difference highlights the healthcare and lifestyle gap across regions.

Life Expectancy by Country

• A bar chart compares countries with the highest and lowest life expectancies.
• Countries like Japan and Switzerland lead the chart, while others fall behind due to lower health investment.

Life Expectancy vs GDP

• High-GDP countries tend to have better life outcomes, but not always — some nations with moderate GDPs still outperform in longevity.
• Low-GDP countries like Liberia and Malawi reveal much shorter life spans, often under 60 years.

Life Expectancy vs BMI

• Separate charts show how high BMI countries (like Samoa and Qatar) often experience lower life expectancy.
• Conversely, countries with low BMI — such as Vietnam and Bangladesh — generally report longer lifespans.

Adult Mortality Trends

• A dynamic line chart tracks mortality across years.
• Countries with persistently high mortality include Zimbabwe and Lesotho, indicating healthcare challenges.

Conclusions

• Lifestyle and economic development clearly influence how long people live — but it's not always a direct correlation.
• Low BMI doesn’t guarantee better health, and high GDP doesn’t always ensure longer life expectancy.
• Health policies, nutrition, access to care, and environmental factors matter greatly.
